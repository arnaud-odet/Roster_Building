\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Data}

We relied on three data sources in this paper: (i) a tracking-improved box-score dataset for identifying players' archetypes (see paragraph \ref{sec:data_trbs}), (ii) play-by-play data to retrieve lineups used by teams \ob{and their relative +/- statistics} (see paragraph \ref{sec:data_pbp}) and (iii) team performance metrics to assess success of teams (see paragraph \ref{sec:data_success}).
We only retain in our analysis complete seasons where these three sources are available, and considered only regular season games. 
Playoff games were discarded, as we believe they could bring a distortion to the analysis.
Indeed, only the best performing teams of a season qualify to the playoffs, facing stronger adversity, which could deteriorate their performance.
Moreover, this choice allows to have exactly the same number of games for all NBA teams, and therefore not over represent good performing teams.
Our dataset covers 5 NBA seasons, from 2015/16 to 2019/20, representing a total of 5,980 games (1,230 per season from 2015/16 to 2018/19 and 1,060 for the 2019/20 season, due to the modification of the season's schedule incurred by the Covid-19 pandemic).

\subsubsection{Tracking-improved box-score data}
\label{sec:data_trbs}

The dataset presented in this paragraph has been referenced in \cite{muniz_weighted_2022} and is publicly available on their \href{https://github.com/meganlynn12/jsa-cdkm?tab=readme-ov-file}{github repository}\footnote{Consulted on February 25\textsuperscript{th}, 2026}.
It consists in data obtained from the website \href{https://www.nba.com/}{nba.com}, initially split in 21 raw datasets and reorganized by the authors of \cite{muniz_weighted_2022} in 6 datasets as described in Table \ref{tab:datasets}.
Please note that contrarily to \citeauthor{muniz_weighted_2022}, we did not consider the "Clutch" dataset as it contains data from particular part of games (less than 5 points difference in the last 5 minutes), and therefore overlaps with other datasets. 
These data contains traditional basketball box-score statistics (e.g. shot attempted, shot made, rebound, assists, etc.) augmented from Sports VU tracking system (introduced in NBA arenas in 2014).
Tracking improved box-score statistics as it (i) brought higher granularity to existing indicators (for instance, categorizing shots by areas on the pitch rather than sole value, identifying if a rebound or a shot is contested or not, etc.) and (ii) created new indicators (by recording for instance the number of boxouts\footnote{A boxout is a situation where a player manages to position himself between the basket and his opponent in a shooting situation, to achieve higher probability of securing the rebound.}).

In these datasets, every entry corresponds to a player's average statistics over a season. 
We concatenated seasons and datasets and removed of duplicated columns (minutes and number of games played are recorded in each dataset).
In total, the concatenated dataset contains 2,519 entries (player's season) distributed into 30 NBA teams, and has 129 dimensions (performance indicators).
We manually excluded features being collinear by design (e.g. $TS_{PCT}$, the True Shooting Percentage, is obtained from other shooting statistics, $AST$ and $TOV$, the total number of assists and turnovers is the sum of the number of assists and turnovers by locations, etc.), which reduced the number of features to 116.
We subsequently scaled our dataset using a \ob{TBD} scaler and further reduced the dimensionality of our dataset by applying a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and retaining 80\% of explained variance.

\subsubsection{Play-by-play data}
\label{sec:data_pbp}

The dataset presented in this section is publicly available \href{https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/schmadam97/nba-playbyplay-data-20182019}{Kaggle}\footnote{Consulted on February 25\textsuperscript{th}, 2026}.
Play-by-play data record timestamp-indexed of event in a game. 
Recorded event encompasses traditional box-scores events (e.g. shot attempts, rebounds, assists, etc.) and the identity of the players credited for the corresponding events, as well as game-related information (start of period, end of period, player substitutions, etc.).
From the identity of players credited for event and the changes log, we were able to identify for all timestamps the composition of both team participating in a game.

\subsubsection{Team performance data}
\label{sec:data_success}
We collected from \href{https://www.nba.com/}{nba.com} two indicators of teams' success:
\begin{enumerate}[i]
  \item Teams' Win Rate (number of games won / number of games played), ranging from 0.122 to 0.890 ($\mu = 0.500, \sigma = 0.152$).
  \item A binary indicator worth 1 if the considered team reached the conference semi-finals in the playoffs and 0 otherwise, as suggested in \cite{ke_unified_2024}. 
  The NBA is split into two conferences (East and West), both having their own playoff competition.
  The NBA champion is the winner of the best-of-seven series between East and West champions.
  Reaching the conference semi-finals is therefore equivalent to being within the last 8 contenders for the NBA title, which corresponds to the top 26.7\%. 
\end{enumerate} 

An illustration of the distribution of success can be found in Figure \ref{fig:success}. 
Top performing teams appear to be the Golden State Warriors 2015, 2016, 2017 and the San Antonio Spurs 2016, achieving win rates above 80\% and reaching Conference semi-finals, while Minnesota Timberwolves 2015 and Philadelphia 76ers 2016 emerge as low performers with win rate below 20\%. 
Atlanta Hawks 2017 and 2019, and Memphis Grizzlies 2017 and 2018 aligned over 700 different lineups during the season, and Minnesota Timberwolves 2017, Orlando Magic 2018 and Detroit Pistons 2015 less than 240. None of these teams reached the Conference semi-finals.

\subsection{Clustering of NBA players}

From the tracking-improved boxscore dataset (see \ref{sec:data_trbs}), we aim at identifying players' archetypes.
To do so, we need to create a partition of our dataset.
We benchmarked various approaches, including centroid-based, hierachical agglomerative, density-based and gaussian models, with a hyperparameters search. 

Among partition-based approaches, the centroid-based k-means algorithm partitions observations into k  groups by minimizing within-cluster dispersion, typically defined as the sum of squared Euclidean distances between observations and their corresponding cluster centroids. 
Hierarchical clustering methods provide an alternative framework by constructing nested partitions without directly optimizing a global objective. 
In particular, agglomerative hierarchical clustering proceeds iteratively by merging pairs of clusters according to a linkage criterion (single, complete, average, or Ward's minimum-variance linkage). 
The resulting dendrogram encodes a multi-scale representation of the data, allowing cluster structure to be examined at different levels of granularity.
Density-based approaches define clusters as connected regions of high sample density. HDBSCAN extends the DBSCAN framework by building a hierarchy based on mutual reachability distances and extracting clusters according to their stability across density levels. 
This formulation enables the identification of clusters with varying densities while treating low-density regions as noise, which is particularly relevant in high-dimensional and irregularly distributed datasets. 
However, in our case, we allocated "noisy" players to the nearest cluster (based on distance to cluster's centroid), as (i) we see no reason to discard outliers and (ii) there is a significant risk high performing (and therefore highly represented) players would be considered as outliers. 
Finally, model-based clustering relies on probabilistic generative assumptions. 
Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) assume that observations arise from a finite mixture of Gaussian distributions, each parameterized by a mean vector and covariance matrix. Model parameters are typically estimated via the EM algorithm, yielding soft probabilistic assignments of observations to latent components.

\paragraph{Comparison metric}
To determine which clustering algorithm yields the best results on our dataset, we need to compare them.
Contrary to supervised learning, where a form of consensus emerges around some key metrics (e.g. Accuracy, F1 score and ROC-AUC for classification tasks, RMSE and its variants for regression tasks), Clusteri Validity Indices (CVIs) are still subject to discussion within the academic community. 
\cite{Arbelaitz2013AnEC} compared 30 CVIs and concluded three groups of indices emerge, and the first of them contains indices that "behave better", with "the differences being statistically significant". 
Well know metrics such as Silhouette Score, Davies-Bouldin, Calinski-Harabasz, etc. are listed as part of this well performing group.
On the other hand, \cite{Gagolewski_2021} stated it is better "not to treat them as objective functions for identifying good partitions", and specified this is particularly the case for Silhouette and Davies-Boulin. 
We therefore chose to rely on the Calinski-Harabasz index to compare different methods.
This metrics is defined as follows:

\begin{equation}
CH = \frac{n-k}{k-1} \times \frac{ \sum_{1}^{n}{||\mu - \mu_{C(i)}||^{2}}}{ \sum_{1}^{n}{||x_i - \mu_{C(i)}||^{2}} } 
\end{equation}

where $n$ is the population size, $k$ the number of clusters, $C(i)$ is the clusters in which point $x_i, i \in \{1, ..., n\}$ is allocated, $\mu$ and $\mu_{C(i)}$ are the centroids of the population and of cluster $C(i)$ respectively, and $|| a - b ||$ represents the Euclidian distance between points $a$ and $b$.
