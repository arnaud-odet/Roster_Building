\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Data}

We relied on three data sources in this work: (i) a tracking-improved box-score dataset for identifying players' archetypes (see paragraph~\ref{sec:data_trbs}), (ii) play-by-play data to retrieve lineups used by teams and their relative +/- statistics (see paragraph~\ref{sec:data_pbp}) and (iii) team performance metrics to assess success of teams (see paragraph~\ref{sec:data_success}).
We only retain in our analysis complete seasons where these three sources are available, and considered only regular season games. 
Playoff games were discarded, as we believe they could bring a distortion to the analysis.
Indeed, only the best performing teams of a season qualify to the playoffs, facing stronger adversity, which could deteriorate their performance.
Moreover, this choice allows to have exactly the same number of games for all NBA teams, and therefore not over represent good performing teams.
Our dataset covers 5 NBA seasons, from 2015/16 to 2019/20, representing a total of 5,980 games (1,230 per season from 2015/16 to 2018/19 and 1,060 for the 2019/20 season, due to the modification of the season's schedule incurred by the Covid-19 pandemic).

\subsubsection{Tracking-improved box-score data}
\label{sec:data_trbs}

The dataset presented in this paragraph has been referenced in \cite{muniz_weighted_2022} and is publicly available on their \href{https://github.com/meganlynn12/jsa-cdkm?tab=readme-ov-file}{github repository}\footnote{Consulted on February 25\textsuperscript{th}, 2026}.
It consists in data obtained from the website \href{https://www.nba.com/}{nba.com}, initially split in 21 raw datasets and reorganized by the authors of \cite{muniz_weighted_2022} in 6 datasets as described in Table~\ref{tab:datasets}.
Please note that contrarily to \citeauthor{muniz_weighted_2022}, we did not consider the "Clutch" dataset as it contains data from particular part of games (less than 5 points difference in the last 5 minutes), and therefore overlaps with other datasets. 
These data contains traditional basketball box-score statistics (e.g. shot attempted, shot made, rebound, assists, etc.) augmented from Sports VU tracking system (introduced in NBA arenas in 2014).
Tracking improved box-score statistics as it (i) brought higher granularity to existing indicators (for instance, categorizing shots by areas on the pitch rather than sole value, identifying if a rebound or a shot is contested or not, etc.) and (ii) created new indicators (by recording for instance the number of boxouts\footnote{A boxout is a situation where a player manages to position himself between the basket and his opponent in a shooting situation, to achieve higher probability of securing the rebound.}).

In these datasets, every entry corresponds to a player's average statistics over a season. 
We concatenated seasons and datasets and removed of duplicated columns (minutes and number of games played are recorded in each dataset).
In total, the concatenated dataset contains 2,519 entries (player's season) distributed into 30 NBA teams, and has 129 dimensions (performance indicators).
We manually excluded features being collinear by design (e.g. $TS_{PCT}$, the True Shooting Percentage, is obtained from other shooting statistics, $AST$ and $TOV$, the total number of assists and turnovers is the sum of the number of assists and turnovers by locations, etc.), and we discarded some ratio features, which reduced the number of features to 74.
We subsequently (i) transformed statistics expressed in volumes in per 48 minutes statistics so players can be compared, (ii) scaled our dataset using a minmax scaler and (iii) further reduced the dimensionality of our dataset by applying a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and retaining 80\% of explained variance.

\subsubsection{Play-by-play data}
\label{sec:data_pbp}

The dataset presented in this section is publicly available \href{https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/schmadam97/nba-playbyplay-data-20182019}{Kaggle}\footnote{Consulted on February 25\textsuperscript{th}, 2026}.
Play-by-play data record timestamp-indexed of event in a game. 
Recorded event encompasses traditional box-scores events (e.g. shot attempts, rebounds, assists, etc.) and the identity of the players credited for the corresponding events, as well as game-related information (start of period, end of period, player substitutions, etc.).
From the identity of players credited for event and the changes log, we were able to identify for all timestamps the composition of both team participating in a game. 
A composition (also referred to as lineup) is a combination of 5 players from the pool of available players.
In the NBA, teams are allowed to register 12 players for a given game, corresponding to $\binom{12}{5} = 792$ possible combinations.
In practice, teams often have more than 12 players in their roster, and the selection of the 12 registered for a game is already a one-out-of-many choices, and teams can also sign players for short-duration contracts, trade them in the course of a season, etc.
Therefore, the figure of 792 possible combinations is no theoretical maximum of the number of lineups.
In total, we identified between 12,759 and 14.761 unique lineups per season.
Teams aligned between 187 and 794 different lineups over the course of a season ($\mu = 454.4, \sigma = 114.7$).  
For each lineup, we computed (i) the time it has played and (ii) its +/- statistics.
The +/- statistic corresponds to the difference of points vs opponents when the analyzed lineup is on the floor, a positive value indicating the lineup outscores its opponent and a negative value the opposite. 
It is a well-used indicator on how much difference makes a player or a combination of players.

\subsection{Clustering of NBA players}
\label{sec:clustering}

From the tracking-improved boxscore dataset (see~\ref{sec:data_trbs}), we aim at identifying players' archetypes.
To do so, we need to create a partition of our dataset.
We benchmarked various approaches, including centroid-based, hierachical agglomerative, density-based and gaussian models, with a hyperparameters search. 
We implemented these approaches with the scikit-learn Python library \citep{scikit-learn}.

Among partition-based approaches, the centroid-based k-means algorithm partitions observations into k  groups by minimizing within-cluster dispersion, typically defined as the sum of squared Euclidean distances between observations and their corresponding cluster centroids. 
Hierarchical clustering methods provide an alternative framework by constructing nested partitions without directly optimizing a global objective. 
In particular, agglomerative hierarchical clustering proceeds iteratively by merging pairs of clusters according to a linkage criterion (single, complete, average, or Ward's minimum-variance linkage). 
The resulting dendrogram encodes a multi-scale representation of the data, allowing cluster structure to be examined at different levels of granularity.
Density-based approaches define clusters as connected regions of high sample density. HDBSCAN extends the DBSCAN framework by building a hierarchy based on mutual reachability distances and extracting clusters according to their stability across density levels. 
This formulation enables the identification of clusters with varying densities while treating low-density regions as noise, which is particularly relevant in high-dimensional and irregularly distributed datasets. 
However, in our case, we allocated "noisy" players to the nearest cluster (based on distance to cluster's centroid), as (i) we see no reason to discard outliers and (ii) there is a significant risk high performing (and therefore highly represented) players would be considered as outliers. 
Finally, model-based clustering relies on probabilistic generative assumptions. 
Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) assume that observations arise from a finite mixture of Gaussian distributions, each parameterized by a mean vector and covariance matrix. Model parameters are typically estimated via the EM algorithm, yielding soft probabilistic assignments of observations to latent components.

\paragraph{Comparison metric}
We compared different clustering algorithms to determine which yields the best results on our dataset.
Contrary to supervised learning, where a form of consensus emerges around some key metrics (e.g. Accuracy, F1 score and ROC-AUC for classification tasks, RMSE and its variants for regression tasks), Clustering Validity Indices (CVIs) are still subject to discussion within the academic community. 
\cite{Arbelaitz2013AnEC} compared 30 CVIs and concluded three groups of indices emerge, and the first of them contains indices that "behave better", with "the differences being statistically significant". 
Well know metrics such as Silhouette Score, Davies-Bouldin, Calinski-Harabasz, etc. are listed as part of this well performing group.
On the other hand, \cite{Gagolewski_2021} stated it is better "not to treat them as objective functions for identifying good partitions", and specified this is particularly the case for Silhouette and Davies-Boulin. 
We therefore chose to rely on the Calinski-Harabasz index to compare different methods.
This metrics is defined as follows:

\begin{equation}
CH = \frac{n-k}{k-1} \times \frac{ \sum_{1}^{n}{||\mu - \mu_{C(i)}||^{2}}}{ \sum_{1}^{n}{||x_i - \mu_{C(i)}||^{2}} } 
\end{equation}

where $n$ is the population size, $k$ the number of clusters, $C(i)$ is the clusters in which point $x_i, i \in \{1, ..., n\}$ is allocated, $\mu$ and $\mu_{C(i)}$ are the centroids of the population and of cluster $C(i)$ respectively, and $|| a - b ||$ represents the Euclidian distance between points $a$ and $b$. 
 
Once an optimal method is identified, we investigated several criterions to identify the best number of clusters:
\begin{enumerate}[i]
  \item Clustering method specific indicators (e.g. K-Means inertia)
  \item Gap Statistic \citep{Tibshirani2001EstimatingTN}, which compares the total within intra-cluster variation for different number of clusters with their expected values under null reference distribution of the data. 
  The estimate of the optimal clusters will be value maximizing the gap statistic (equivalently, yielding the largest gap statistic), corresponding to the clustering structure the farthest away from the random uniform distribution of points.
  Algorithm~\ref{alg:gs} summarizes this approach.
\end{enumerate}


\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Gap Statistic (from \citeauth{Tibshirani2001EstimatingTN})}
\footnotesize
\label{alg:gs}
\begin{algorithmic}

  \Statex \textbf{Input:} Data matrix $\{x_{ij}\}$, $i = 1, \ldots, n$, $j = 1, \ldots, p$; 
  \Statex \hspace{3em} Maximum number of clusters $k_{\max}$; 
  \Statex \hspace{3em} Number of reference data sets $B$.
  \Statex \textbf{Output:} Optimal number of clusters $\hat{k}$.

\State $\hat{k} \gets None $
\For{$k = 1, \ldots, k_{\max}$}
  \While{$\hat{k} = None$}
    \State Cluster the observed data and compute the within-cluster dispersion:

    $W_k = \sum_{r=1}^{k} \frac{1}{2n_r} D_r \quad \text{where } D_r = \sum_{a,b' \in C_r} ||a - b||$
    \For{$b = 1, \ldots, B$}
        \State Generate a reference data set $X^*$ from a uniform null distribution.
        \State Cluster $X^*$ and compute $W_{kb}^*$.
        \EndFor
      \State Compute the Gap statistic and its corrected standard deviation:
      $$\mathrm{Gap}(k) = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{b=1}^{B} \log(W_{kb}^*) - \log(W_k), 
      \qquad s_k = \sqrt{1 + \frac{1}{B}} \cdot \mathrm{sd}(k)$$
      \If{$\mathrm{Gap}(k) \geq \mathrm{Gap}(k+1) - s_{k+1}$}
        \State $\hat{k} = k$ 
      \EndIf
  \EndWhile
\EndFor

\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


\subsection{Lineups analysis}
\label{sec:lineup_analysis}

From the lineups identified (see Section~\ref{sec:data_pbp}), we retain for further analysis only those aligned more than 12 minutes (which corresponds the duration of a quarter of a NBA game).
Applying this filter corresponds to keeping 9,666 lineups out of the 68,160 lineups identified (14.2\%), representing 66.9\% of playtime.
A brief description of lineups and their usage in terms of playtime can be found in Table~\ref{tab:lus}.
We computed for all lineups (i) the time they have played and (ii) their +/- statistics.
We subsequently computed the average +/- per 48 minutes.
From the partition of NBA players performed (see Section~\ref{sec:clustering}), we characterize each lineup by its composition in terms of clusters.
Each lineup can be expressed as $L_C = \{k : \text{count}(C(p_i) = k), i \in L_P \forall k \in \{1, ..., n_k\}\}$ where $L_C$ (respectively $L_P$) is the lineups characterized in terms of clusters (respectively players), $C(p_i)$ indicates the clusters in which belongs player $p_i$ and $n_k$ the number of clusters of the selected partition.

Our objective is to find the best compositions in terms of clusters, $L^*_C$.
We note $S(L)$ the performance of lineup $L$ and define the performance of a given lineup as its +/- per 48 minutes.
We want $L^*_C$ to be the solution of Equation~\ref{eq:opt} given constraint presented in Equation~\ref{eq:contraints}. 

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:opt}
  S(L^*_C) = \max_{L \in \Omega_L} S(L)  
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:contraints}
  L = \{k : c_k, k \in \{1, ..., n_k\}\}\quad \text{with} \quad \sum_{k = 1}^{n_k} c_k = 5
\end{equation}

where $\Omega_L$ is the universe of possible compositions and $c_k$ represents the number of players of cluster $k$ present in the lineup.

For every combination of $n_p$ players, $n_p \in \{1, ..., 5\}$, we assessed the performance of the combination $S\left(\bigcap (C(P_1), ... , C(P_{n_p}))\right)$, where $C: \Omega_P \mapsto \Omega_C$ is the function associating player $P$ to the cluster $c$ it belongs to, $\Omega_P$ and $\Omega_C$ represents respectively the universe of players and clusters as described in Algorithm~\ref{alg:bootstrap}.

\begin{algorithm}
  \caption{Combination performance quantification}
  \footnotesize
  \label{alg:bootstrap}
  \begin{algorithmic}
  
    \Statex \textbf{Input:} Dataset $D$; 
    \Statex \hspace{3em} Combinations players $\{C(p_1), ... , C(p_{n_p}) \}$.
    \Statex \hspace{3em} Validity threshold $n_0$; 
    \Statex \hspace{3em} Number of bootstrapped dataset $n_B$.
    \Statex \hspace{3em} Significance level $\alpha$.
    \Statex \textbf{Output:} Performance of the combination $\hat{S}$.
  
  \State $\hat{S} \gets None $
  \State Partition $D$ into $D_w$ with lineups containing $\{C(p_1), ... , C(p_{n_p}) \}$ and $D_{\bar{w}}$ without.
  \State $n_w \gets \text{size}(D_w) $
  \If {$n_w >= n_0$}
    Create biased distributions $\tilde{D_w}$ and $\tilde{D_{\bar{w}}}$ of $D_w$ and $D_{\bar{w}}$ where all observation are weighted by time spent playing,
    $\Delta_{observed} = \bar{\tilde{D_w}} - \bar{\tilde{D_{\bar{w}}}}$
    \For{$k = 1, \ldots, n_B$}
      \State Generate two bootstrapped datasets $B_w$ from $\tilde{D_w}$ and $B_{\bar{w}}$ from $\tilde{D_{\bar{w}}}$ 
      \State Compute $\Delta_k = \bar{B_w} - \bar{B_{\bar{w}}}$ 
    \EndFor
    \State Compute $p$-value on $\Delta - \Delta_{observed}$
    \If {$p$-value < $\alpha$}
      \State $\hat{S} = \Delta_{observed}$
    \EndIf
  \EndIf
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

In practice, we set $n_B$ to 1000, $\alpha$ to 0.05, $n_0$ to $\min (30, 0.1 \times N)$ where $N$ is our population size.
