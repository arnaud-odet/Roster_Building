\subsubsection{Clustering algorithms}

The Calinski-Harabasz indices of the compared method are presented in Figure~\ref{fig:cl_ch}.
Please note that the number of clusters of for some algorithms (e.g. K-Means, GMM) an input hyperparameter, but for some others (e.g. Partition - HDBSCAN), it is a consequence of other hyperparameters.
A clear hierarchy emerges between algorithm : K-Means, Spherical K-Means and GMM consistently achieve top performance, while Agglomerative with Complete and Average linkage emerge as worst performers.
Agglomerative with Ward linkage consistently falls below the top three performers and Partition HDBSCAN present more variations with cluster sizes, performing at the same level as GMM for the number of clusters for which it ranks best, and as bad as Agglomerative with complete linkage for the others.
From this analysis, we will use K-Means as partition algorithm for the remaining of the paper.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{clustering_ch.png}  
  \caption{Comparison of clustering algorithm.}
  \label{fig:cl_ch}
\end{figure}

For determining the optimal number of clusters, we relied on (i) an elbow method based on the value of K-Means inertia defined as 
\begin{equation}
  Inertia = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sum_{x \in C_i}||x - \mu_{C_i}||^{2} 
\end{equation}
where $\mu_{C_i}$ represents the centroid of the clusters containing $x$, and $k$ the number of clusters,  and (ii) the gap statistic method \citep{Tibshirani2001EstimatingTN}.
For the latter, we ran 100 iterations of Algorithm~\ref{alg:gs} with $B = 10$.
Figure~\ref{fig:cl_ncl} displays the results for both approaches.
From the elbow method, the optimal number of clusters is between 6 and 13 and from the gapstat algorithms, it appears between 9 and 11.
We therefore chose 10 clusters for the selected partition, as 10 is the mode of our gapstat optimal number of clusters and falls within the Elbow method range.

\subsubsection{Selected partition}

Figure~\ref{fig:cl_viz} presents a graphical display of the selected partition, and Table~\ref{tab:clust} a description of the clusters as well as notable players. 
For an extensive clusters' centroid position in Principal Component space (respectively, feature space), please refer to Table~\ref{tab:app_clust} (respectively Table~\ref{tab:app_cl_ft}) in appendix.
From Table~\ref{tab:clust}, we can detect two clusters of "superstar" players: cluster 5 with star guards / small forwards, with high shooting statistics and playmaking, high usage rate, but relatively low rebounding and defensive impact, and cluster 10 with two-ways impacting "bigs" (Power Forward - PF and Centers - C), presenting the highest playmaking characteristics among clusters containing PFs and Cs, good rebounding and defensive impact (even if player 4 and 9 outperform them in the latter).
The remaining clusters contain players who could be regarded as "supporting cast".
Cluster 1 players display high shooting percentages and touch the ball less often. 
They play a rather high number of minutes, and participate in the rebounding tasks.
Cluster 2 contains players with predilection shooting position (Elbow and 3 pointers), with low ball handling and playmaking statistics.
Cluster 3 players participate in the game creation with ball handling, assists, and passes, display good own-shoot (drive and pull-ups) capacity, but little rebounding.*
Players of cluster 4 present very high rebounding and defense statistics, participate in play development with screens and touches / assists from the paint, and display rather good shooting statistics from specific location (post and paint).
Cluster 6 players shoot from the 3 point range, are average in terms of defense and rebounding, and low in terms of ball possession and playmaking.
Players from cluster 7 dribble more than the average player, display low shooting, defensive and rebounding statistics.
Cluster 8 contains big men with good shooting capacity from the post, the paint and the elbow, but bad percentages on own-shoot creation. 
They participate in playmaking with assists from the post and the elbow, and display good rebounding capacity.
Compared to cluster 8's, players from cluster 9 are big men with higher defensive impact, who participate in the play development with their screens. 
They also display good rebounding statistics.
