\subsubsection{Clustering algorithms}

The Calinski-Harabasz indices of the compared method are presented in Figure~\ref{fig:cl_ch}.
Please note that the number of clusters of for some algorithms (e.g. K-Means, GMM) an input hyperparameter, but for some others (e.g. Partition - HDBSCAN), it is a consequence of other hyperparameters.
A clear hierarchy emerges between algorithm : K-Means, Spherical K-Means and GMM consistently achieve top performance, while Agglomerative with Complete and Average linkage emerge as worst performers.
Agglomerative with Ward linkage consistently falls below the top three performers and Partition HDBSCAN present more variations with cluster sizes, performing at the same level as GMM for the number of clusters for which it ranks best, and as bad as Agglomerative with complete linkage for the others.
From this analysis, we will use K-Means as partition algorithm for the remaining of the paper.

\begin{figure}
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{clustering_ch.png}  
  \caption{Comparison of clustering algorithm.}
  \label{fig:cl_ch}
\end{figure}

For determining the optimal number of clusters, we relied on (i) an elbow method based on the value of K-Means inertia defined as 
\begin{equation}
  Inertia = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sum_{x \in C_i}||x - \mu_{C_i}||^{2} 
\end{equation}
where $\mu_{C_i}$ represents the centroid of the clusters containing $x$, and $k$ the number of clusters,  and (ii) the gap statistic method \citep{Tibshirani2001EstimatingTN}.
For the latter, we ran 100 iterations of Algorithm~\ref{alg:gs} with $B = 10$.
Figure~\ref{fig:cl_ncl} displays the results for both approaches.
From the elbow method, the optimal number of clusters is between 6 and 13 and from the gapstat algorithms, it appears between 9 and 11.
We therefore chose 10 clusters for the selected partition, as 10 is the mode of our gapstat optimal number of clusters and falls within the Elbow method range.

\subsubsection{Selected partition}
\label{sec:partition}

Figure~\ref{fig:cl_viz} presents a graphical display of the selected partition, and Table~\ref{tab:clust} a description of the clusters as well as notable players. 
For an extensive clusters' centroid position in Principal Component space (respectively, feature space), please refer to Table~\ref{tab:app_clust} (respectively Table~\ref{tab:app_cl_ft}) in appendix.
From Table~\ref{tab:clust}, we can detect two clusters of "superstar" players: cluster 5 with star guards / small forwards, with high shooting statistics and playmaking, high usage rate, but relatively low rebounding and defensive impact, and cluster 10 with two-ways impacting "bigs" (Power Forward - PF and Centers - C), presenting the highest playmaking characteristics among clusters containing PFs and Cs, good rebounding and defensive impact (even if player 4 and 9 outperform them in the latter).
The remaining clusters contain players who could be regarded as "supporting cast".
Cluster 1 players display high shooting percentages and touch the ball less often. 
They play a rather high number of minutes, and participate in the rebounding tasks.
Cluster 2 contains players with predilection shooting position (Elbow and 3 pointers), with low ball handling and playmaking statistics.
Cluster 3 players participate in the game creation with ball handling, assists, and passes, display good own-shoot (drive and pull-ups) capacity, but little rebounding.*
Players of cluster 4 present very high rebounding and defense statistics, participate in play development with screens and touches / assists from the paint, and display rather good shooting statistics from specific location (post and paint).
Cluster 6 players shoot from the 3 point range, are average in terms of defense and rebounding, and low in terms of ball possession and playmaking.
Players from cluster 7 dribble more than the average player, display low shooting, defensive and rebounding statistics.
Cluster 8 contains big men with good shooting capacity from the post, the paint and the elbow, but bad percentages on own-shoot creation. 
They participate in playmaking with assists from the post and the elbow, and display good rebounding capacity.
Compared to cluster 8's, players from cluster 9 are big men with higher defensive impact, who participate in the play development with their screens. 
They also display good rebounding statistics.

\subsection{Lineups analysis}

From the clustering of players and the subsequent characterization of lineups, we identified 556 unique lineups (from 9,666 when players were considered instead of clusters).
Table~\ref{tab:lineup_comb} displays the number of possible combinations of clusters and the number of valid possibilities in our dataset (where a candidate lineup is considered as valid when $n \geq n_0$, $n$ being the sample size associated with the lineup and $n_0$ the threshold defined in Section~\ref{sec:lineup_analysis}).
Intuitively, as the combination size increases, the percentage of valid lineups decreases, starting for 100\% for combination of one player to 4\% for combination of five players.
In total, we identified 140 combinations statistically significant and valid. 
We also reported the number of statistically significant combinations, without consideration of their validity, for further analysis.  
We do not comment those results in this section, as bootstrapping results are not reliable when sample sizes are too small.

Figure~\ref{fig:cl_l1-2} displays the +/- per 48 minutes statistic depending on the presence or absence of clusters and pairs of clusters (i.e. combinations size 1 and 2), and Table~\ref{tab:app_comb} in Appendix presents the valid and statistically significant combination of three, four and five players.
Please have in mind that for any combination of $n$ players where $n < 5$, it is possible to find a combination of $n+1$ players from which the conclusion might differ.
As an illustration, we can see in Figure~\ref{fig:cl_l1-2} lineups with players from cluster~8 significantly underperform lineups without players from cluster~8 (left panel), yet lineups with players from cluster~5 and cluster~8 significantly overperform lineup without this particular cluster pair (right panel).
In that case, concluding players from cluster~8 are to be avoided when building a lineup would be erroneous.  

\paragraph{Presence or absence of clusters:}
We identified two clusters from which lineups containing these clusters significantly outperform lineup without: cluster~5 and cluster~10, the two described as "superstars" clusters in Section~\ref{sec:partition}.
Cluster~5 brings a +3.5~pts per 48 minutes improvement vs lineups without cluster~5, and this statistic for cluster~10 is +2.2~pts per 48 minutes. 
On the other hand, four clusters significantly decrease lineups +/- per 48 minutes: cluster~7 by -3.5~pts, cluster~3 by -2.2~pts, cluster~8 by -1.0~pts and cluster~4 by -0.8~pts.
Interestingly, cluster~9 (-1.1~pts) and cluster~1 (-0.9~pts) display mean difference lower than cluster~4 but are not statistically significant.

\paragraph{Presence or absence of cluster pairs:}
We identified 19 combinations of players as valid and significant.
Unsurprisingly, cluster~5 and cluster~10 appear in more statistically significant positive impact combination, with six for cluster~5 and four for cluster~10.
Interestingly, no combinations involving cluster~5 and another cluster~display a higher mean difference than cluster~5 alone.
Indeed, as indicated previously, lineups with at least one cluster~5 players outperform lineups without by +3.5~pts while the highest mean difference of pairs of clusters involving cluster~5 is +3.4~pts (for two cluster~5 players and one cluster~5 plus one cluster~10).
However, the cluster~9 and cluster~10 pair lineups significantly outperform lineups where this pair is not present by +7.8~pts, the highest score displayed.
Cluster~9 and 10 both are clusters with a majority of "bigs" (Center and Power Forwards), and while cluster~10 regroups "superstar" bigs, cluster~9 is more a cluster~of role player bigs, impacting the game with their defense, rebounding and ability to play with screens.
This result highlight the importance of compatibility in the paint (the area located close to the basket, where the bigs usually play) over superstar associations (lineups with two players from cluster~10's results are close to zero and non-significant).
Cluster~9 and cluster~10 associations were notably aligned by the 2016/17 San Antonio Spurs (Dewayne Dedmon and LaMarcus Aldridge), the 2016/17 and 2018/19 Golden State Warriors (Damian Jones and Draymond Green and JaVale McGee and Draymond Green), the 2018/19 Bosto Celtics (Aron Baynes and Al Horford) and the 2016/17 New Orelans Pelicans (Omer Asik adn Anthony Davis).

\paragraph{Three players combinations:}
Comprehensive results for three players combinations are displayed in Appendix, Table~\ref{tab:app_comb_3}.
We identified 41 triplets significantly underperforming (20) or overperforming (21) others.
In line with our two players combination findings, the cluster~9 - cluster~10 pair appears in the top two triplets (with cluster~5 for a +10.3~pts per 48 minutes gap and with cluster~1 for a +9.8~pts difference.)
Notably, five triplets including a pair of cluster~5 players appear in positive impact triplets (completed with cluster~3, 10, 2, 1 and 4).
We also observe that only one triplet without player for cluster~5 or 10 is listed in the good performing triplets (3-3-4 with +2.7~pts). 
In the low performing combination, only three contain a superstar cluster player (3-8-10 with a -2.9~pts per 48 minutes difference, 5-8-8 with -3.2~pts and 4-4-5 with -3.7~pts).
This analysis confirms the dominance of superstar clusters.

\paragraph{Four players combinations:}
Comprehensive results for four players combinations are displayed in Appendix, Table~\ref{tab:app_comb_4}.
We identified 47 combinations significantly underperforming (21) or overperforming (26) others.
For top performing lineups, the gap between lineups who contain the combination and lineups who do not is reduced compared to combination of three players.
Indeed, while this gap reaches +10.3~pts for triplets, the top value for four players combination is "only" +7.1~pts.  
This is explained by the small sample sizes of top performing triplets (respectively 37 and 34 for triplets including clusters~9 and 10, none of them appear in a valid 4 players combination). 
Out of 26 overperforming combinations, 10 do not meet the "superteam" definition we suggested in Section~\ref{sec:intro}, 13 contain two players of superstar clusters, and only 3 contain three players from superstar clusters.
Interestingly, the top lineup cannot be considered as a superteam.
On the other hand, no lineup significantly underperforming can be identified as superteam.


\paragraph{Five players lineups:}
We identified 82 valid combinations of players, of which 26 statistically significantly outperform (13) or underperform (13) other lineups.
Please refer to Table~\ref{tab:app_comb_5} in Appendix for valid and statistically significant combinations.
Once again, the top performing lineup can not be considered as a superteam. 
It is composed by players from clusters 1, 2, 3, 5 and 8, one occurrence of each cluster, and outperform other lineups by +10.2~pts.
In our dataset, the lineup corresponding to this combination that played most minutes (297) and generated highest +/- (+101) was used by the Toronto Raptors in 2015/16 (68.3\% win rate, conference finals).
It was composed by Cory Joseph (cluster~3) and Kyle Lowry (cluster~5) as guards, Terrence Ross (cluster~1) and Patrick Patterson (cluster~2) as forwards and Bismack Biyombo (cluster~8) as center.
Most positive impact lineups can be considered as superteam, 3 of them with three superstars and 7 of them with two superstars, while only 3 lineups with one or less player from superstar clusters appear in this category.
For negative impact lineups, only two of them contains two players from superstar cluster.
